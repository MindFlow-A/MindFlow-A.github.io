Our Research

The history of artificial intelligence began in the 20th century, but its concept dates back to Ancient Greece (12th century BCE to 4th century CE).
At that time, there were already discussions about the possibility of creating an artificial entity capable of performing human tasks.
Although the earliest machines were designed for military purposes, they reinforced the idea that engineering could be capable of carrying out
complex tasks like a human. Then, in 1943, psychologist Walter Pitts and cybernetics specialist Warren McCulloch proposed a simplified
mathematical model to illustrate how the human brain works. This concept laid the foundation for studies on algorithms and deep learning.

Here are some aplications of AI in our society:

Health: Faster and more accurate diagnoses, as well as robot-assisted surgeries, which increase precision.
Industry: Higher production.
Transport: Autonomous cars (Tesla) use AI for safe navigation.
Commerce: Chatbots provide 24-hour customer service.

AI in Psychotherapy and Mental Health

AI-powered chatbots and virtual therapists are becoming more prevalent, providing users with accessible mental health support.
For instance, platforms like Character.ai offer bots that simulate therapy sessions, making mental health assistance available 24/7.

AI and Diagonostic in Psychology

AI algorithms analyze data such as text, images, and speech patterns to assist in diagnosing mental disorders, including depression and schizophrenia.
AI models have achieved up to 92% accuracy in predicting suicide attempts and highlighting their potential in critical mental health interventions.

AI and Emotion Recognition

According to the World Health Organization, nowadays, AI systems are being developed to analyze facial expressions, voice tones,
and behaviors to understand emotional states, supporting psychological interventions. AI is progressing even more towards
recognizing emotions through voice analysis and gesture recognition, aiming to interpret the emotions they represent.
Besides that, ensuring accuracy in diverse cultural contexts and individual differences remains a significant challenge.

According to the American Psychological Association, when it comes to AI, "psychologists have the expertise to question assumptions
about new technology and examine its impact on users". Psychologists apear to be taking close attention to the interaction
between humans and machines to understand how people perceive AI and what ripple effects such perceptions could have across
our modern society. A study found that "people are less morally outraged by gender discrimination caused by an algorithm
as opposed to discrimination created by humans." Which can be scary, and may increase the level of discrimination
in the world, maybe even destroying all the progress in that sector.

Our Opinion:

AI has significant potential to enhance mental health care by making psychological support more accessible and efficient.
However, its implementation must be carefully managed to address ethical issues, ensure patient privacy, and maintain the human element of therapy.
AI should serve as a complementary tool, augmenting traditional psychological care rather than replacing it.
